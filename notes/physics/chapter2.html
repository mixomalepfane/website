< Chapter 2: Motion Along a Straight Line

One purpose of physics is to study the motion of objects—how fast they move and how far they travel in a given amount of time. NASCAR engineers are fanatical about this aspect of physics as they determine the performance of their cars before and during a race. In this chapter, we study motion along a single axis, called one-dimensional motion.

2-1 Position, Displacement, and Average Velocity

Position

The position \(x\) of a particle on an \(x\)-axis locates it with respect to the origin (zero point). Position is positive or negative depending on the side of the origin, or zero if at the origin. The positive direction is toward increasing values; the opposite is the negative direction. Position is measured in meters (m).

Displacement

The displacement \(\Delta x\) is the change in position: \[\Delta x = x_2 - x_1\] Displacement is a vector quantity and measured in meters (m).

Average Velocity

If a particle moves from \(x_1\) to \(x_2\) over \(\Delta t = t_2 - t_1\), its average velocity is: \[v_{avg} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{t_2 - t_1}\] The sign of \(v_{avg}\) indicates direction. Average velocity depends on initial and final positions, not path length.

On an \(x\) vs. \(t\) graph, average velocity equals the slope of the straight line connecting the two endpoints. It is measured in meters per second (m·s-1).

Average Speed

Average speed \(s_{avg}\) is based on the total distance traveled over time: \[s_{avg} = \frac{total\ distance}{\Delta t}\] It is a scalar, measured in meters per second (m·s-1).

2-2 Instantaneous Velocity and Speed

Instantaneous velocity \(v\) is the rate of change of position at a specific instant: \[v = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx}{dt}\]

Speed is the magnitude of velocity. Both are measured in meters per second (m·s-1).

2-3 Acceleration

Average Acceleration

Defined as the change in velocity over time: \[a_{avg} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}\] where \(v_1\) is the velocity at time \(t_1\) and \(v_2\) at time \(t_2\). Measured in meters per second squared (m·s-2).

Instantaneous Acceleration

The instantaneous acceleration (or simply acceleration) \(a\) is the rate at which velocity is changing at that instant. \[a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^{\ 2}x}{dt^{\ 2}}\]

2-4 Constant Acceleration

The following equations describe motion under constant acceleration:

\[v = v_0 + at\] \[v^{\ 2} = v_0^{\ 2} + 2a\ (x - x_0)\] \[x - x_0 = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^{\ 2}\] \[x - x_0 = \frac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)t\] \[x - x_0 = vt - \frac{1}{2}at^{\ 2}\]

These equations apply only when acceleration is constant.

2-5 FREE-FALL ACCELERATION

An important example of straight-line motion with constant acceleration is that of an object rising or falling freely near Earth’s surface. The constant acceleration equations describe this motion, but we make two changes in notation:

  1. We refer the motion to the vertical \(y\) axis with \(+y\) vertically up.
  2. We replace \(a\) with \(-g\), where \(g\) is the magnitude of the free-fall acceleration. Near Earth’s surface, \(g = 9.8\) m·s-2.

Note: free-fall acceleration near Earth’s surface is \(a = -g = -9.8\) m·s-2, and the magnitude of the acceleration is \(g = 9.8\) m·s-2. Do not substitute \(-9.8\) m·s-2 for \(g\) .
