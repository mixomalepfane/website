Chapter 2: Motion Along a Straight Line

One purpose of physics is to study the motion of objects, how fast they move and how far they move in a given amount of time. NASCAR engineers are fanatical about this aspect of physics as they determine the performance of their cars before and during a race. In this chapter, we study the basic physics of motion where the object moves along a single axis. Such motion is called one-dimensional motion.

2-1 POSITION, DISPLACEMENT, AND AVERAGE VELOCITY

Position

The position \(x\) of a particle on an \(x\) axis locates the particle with respect to the origin, or zero point, of the axis.The position is either positive or negative, according to which side of the origin the particle is on, or zero if the particle is at the origin. The positive direction on an axis is the direction of increasing positive numbers, the opposite direction is the negative direction on the axis. Position is measured in meters ( \(m\) ).

Displacement

The displacement \(\Delta x\) of a particle is the change in its position: \[\Delta x = x_2 - x_1 \]

Displacement is a vector quantity. It is positive if the particle has moved in the positive direction of the \(x\) axis and negative if the particle has moved in the negative direction. Dispalcement is measured in meters ( \(m\) ).

Average Velocity

When a particle has moved from position \(x_1\) to position \(x_2\) during a time interval \(\Delta t = t_2 - t_1\), its average velocity during that interval is: \[v_{avg} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{t_2 - t_1}\]

The algebraic sign of \(v_{avg}\) indicates the direction of motion (\(v_{avg}\) is a vector quantity). Average velocity does not depend on the actual distance a particle moves, but instead depends on its original and final positions.

On a graph of \(x\) versus \(t\) , the average velocity for a time interval \(\Delta t\) is the slope of the straight line connecting the points on the curve that represent the two ends of the interval. Avarage velocity is measured in meters per second ( \(m.s^{\ -1}\) ).

Average Speed

The average speed \(s_{avg}\) of a particle during a time interval \(\Delta t\) depends on the total distance the particle moves in that time interval: \[s_{avg} = \frac{total\ distance}{\Delta t}\]
Average speed does not include direction. It is measured in meters per second ( \(m.s^{\ -1}\) ).

2-2 INSTANTANEOUS VELOCITY AND SPEED

The instantaneous velocity (or simply velocity) \(v\) refers to how fast a particle is moving at a given instant: \[v = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx}{dt}\] where the displacement \(\Delta x = x_2 - x_1\) and time interval \(\Delta t = t_2 - t_1\) .

Speed is the magnitude of instantaneous velocity. Both speed and velocity are measured in meters per second ( \(m.s^{\ -1}\) ) .

2-3 ACCELERATION

When a particle’s velocity changes, the particle is said to undergo acceleration (or to accelerate).

Average acceleration

Average acceleration is the ratio of a change in velocity \(\Delta v\) to the time interval \(\Delta t\) in which the change occurs. \[a_{avg} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}\] where the particle has velocity \(v_1\) at time \(t_1\) and then velocity \(v_2\) at time \(t_2\) . Avarage velocity is measured in meters per second squared ( \(m.s^{\ -2}\) ).

Instantaneous acceleration

Instantaneous acceleration (or simply acceleration) \(a\) is the rate at which its velocity is changing at that instant. \[a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^{\ 2}x}{dt^{\ 2}}\]

2-4 CONSTANT ACCELERATION

The following five equations describe the motion of a particle with constant acceleration: \[v = v_0 + at\] \[v^{\ 2} = v_{0}^{\ 2} + 2 a\ (x - x_0)\] \[x - x_0 = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} at^{\ 2}\] \[x - x_0 = \frac{1}{2} (v_0 + v)t\] \[x - x_0 = vt - \frac{1}{2}at^{\ 2}\] These equations are not valid when the acceleration is not constant.

2-5 FREE-FALL ACCELERATION

An important example of straight-line motion with constant acceleration is that of an object rising or falling freely near Earth’s surface. The constant acceleration equations describe this motion, but we make two changes in notation:

(1) we refer the motion to the vertical \(y\) axis with \(+y\) vertically up.

(2) we replace \(a\) with \(-g\), where \(g\) is the magnitude of the free-fall acceleration. Near Earth’s surface, \(g = 9.8 m.s^{\ 2}\).

Note: free-fall acceleration near Earth’s surface is \(a = -g = -9.8 \ m.s^{\ -2}\) , and the magnitude of the acceleration is
\(g = 9.8 \ m.s^{\ -2}\) . Do not substitute \(-9.8\ m.s^{\ -2}\) for \(g\) .

2-6 GRAPHICAL INTEGRATION IN MOTION ANALYSIS

On a graph of acceleration \(a\) versus time \(t\) , the change in the velocity is given by

\(v_1 - v_0 = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} a \ dt\)

The integral amounts to finding an area on the graph:

\( \int_{t_0}^{t_1} a \ dt = (\) area between acceleration curve and time axis, from \(t_0\) to \(t_1)\)

This area gives the change in velocity.

On a graph of velocity \(v\) versus time \(t\) , the change in the position is given by

\(x_1 - x_0 = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} v \ dt\)

The integral amounts to finding an area on the graph:

\( \int_{t_0}^{t_1} v \ dt = (\) area between velocity curve and time axis, from \(t_0\) to \(t_1)\)

This area gives the change in position.
