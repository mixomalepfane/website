< About Me

Hello, and welcome to my website. My name is Mixo Malepfane, and I'm a programmer from South Africa with a strong interest in computer networks, databases and operating systems. I studied Computer Science and Mathematics at North-West University.

My passion for computers began with video games like Grand Theft Auto, FIFA, and Need for Speed. I was fascinated by how developers created such immersive virtual worlds-experiences that kept me engaged for hours. Although I had access to computers from a young age, I only started programming when I got to university.

At the time, my degree didn’t include courses on 3D graphics or game development. Curious and determined, I turned to the internet and began learning OpenGL through the open.gl website. I explored game development for a while but quickly realized that it was a massive endeavor requiring collaboration across many specialized areas. Still, I enjoyed rendering 3D objects for fun. It also helped reinforce the mathematical concepts I was learning in class.

In my third year, I took courses in operating systems and computer networks. That’s when I began to appreciate how interconnected software really is. Most software isn’t meaningful in isolation, it's the networked experience that keeps users engaged. I decided to focus more on networking. However, I struggled with the university's networking module and passed mostly by memorizing material. Wanting a deeper understanding, I picked up Internetworking with TCP/IP by Douglas Comer. It is one of the best books I've ever read.

As I dug deeper into the TCP/IP protocol suite, I found myself forgetting earlier concepts. A common issue when you read a book and then move on without applying it. To truly understand, I turned to TCP/IP Illustrated, Volume 2: The Implementation by W. Richard Stevens and Gary R. Wright. I decided to implement the protocols described in the book.

But implementing these protocols required a solid understanding of operating systems and kernel-level privileges. So, I began writing a small operating system kernel. It is based on FreeBSD, a descendent of the 4.4BSD-Lite Unix system used in the book.
